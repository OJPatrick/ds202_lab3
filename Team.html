5. A perplexing feature from this specific exploration is when looking at the “No answer” response. It is small group of only 27 responses, however their mean and median values are considerably higher than all other groups. Also, their standard deviation is lowest in comparison to other responses. With the total responses being low, this outcome is not hard to believe, but it is still an interesting result. The response with the second highest mean in education was “Never married”. This is a believable result, because people who are not married will likely have more freedom to further their education more so than people who are married who would likely make education decisions based around other people’s lives, their spouse and possibly children, before their own.

  1. It is not surprising that respondents who answer “25000 or more” for their income appear to typically have the largest income typically. This is due to multiple reasons in my opinion. First, more affluent families typically push their children to further their education and they usually have the resources to support them more financially than less affluent families. Second, more people have been attending university in the last few decades. Given that the typical incomes of this last decade or so is greater than what is was 30 years or more ago, that would lead to more respondents falling into the “25000 or more” response. It is interesting that the “Not applicable” response was used most frequently. The “Not applicable” response has the lowest mean education in years. This could be due to less educated respondents not feeling secure in disclosing their income bracket or that they don’t feel that that is something others should know about.
